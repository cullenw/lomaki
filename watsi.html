Why Lomaki Picked Watsi

Healthcare is a basic human right. Every person on Earth should have access to medical treatment, but the sad reality is that millions of people are unable to get the care they need. Watsi is changing that, by leveraging technology to connect those who need help with those who can provide it. Watsi is rigorously committed to impact, innovation, efficiency, and transparency.

Overview

Watsi is a crowdfunding platform that lets you directly fund low-cost, high-impact medical treatments for people in need where 100% of donations go directly to patients.

The Work

People featured on Watsi are afflicted with conditions that, if left untreated, will severely impact their standard of living. Treatments eligible for funding cost less than $1500, have a high probability of success, and are financially out-of-reach for the patient in need. The site lets anyone contribute as little as $5 to help these people receive the care they need.

"Watsi believes that every person deserves access to medical care, regardless of their nationality, religion, or socioeconomic status. We envision a world where people no longer die from conditions that society has the knowledge, resources, and power to treat."

Your Impact

Watsi donations help people like Lilia, a 30 year old mother from the Philippines who needs a hernia repair and caesarian section to safely deliver her second child; and Prince, an 11 year old child from Ghana who will be crippled by scoliosis without surgery.

Watsi uses 100% of every donation of fund medical care. The Universal Fund is Watsi's general fund -- rather than donating to a specific person, you enable Watsi to allocate your donation for you. This is particularly important for time-sensitive cases, patients who need emergency care, or those that don't wish to be featured on the site.

The Story

Founder Chase Adams served in the Peace Corps in Costa Rica. He was on a bus where a woman was collecting donations from passengers to fund treatment for her son. Chase realized that if he could connect this woman with people in the U.S., she would raise enough within the day.

"In that moment I had what can only be described as an epiphany. If I could somehow connect this woman with my friends and family back home, she would have the money to pay for her son's medical treatment within the day." Chase Adams.

Adams returned to the U.S. and founded Watsi in 2012. It became the first ever nonprofit accepted into Y Combinator.
